Phase 01 · PDF Formula
Connection
Shift focus entirely to the player. Establish psychological safety before any correction. A player who doesn't feel heard won't execute. This is the most skipped phase in all of coaching — and the most costly.
Key rule: You are genuinely curious, not correcting anything yet. Be a problem-finder. The player must feel you're on their side before they'll take direction.
Time allocation: 15–20% of your total communication window. Even in a 30-second sideline pull, the first 6 seconds must be connection — not instruction.
PDF principle: Prospects (players) persuade themselves. Your opening question determines whether they're open or closed for the next 8 phases.
Halftime · Connection Scripts
"Before we get into adjustments — what's feeling off out there to you right now?"
"Walk me through what you're seeing from your position. What's different from what we prepared for?"
"I saw a few things out there — what did you notice?"
Sideline Pull · 30-Second Version
"Hey — what are you seeing on that side of the field?"
"Talk to me. What's happening out there?"
Phase 02 · PDF Formula
Situation Read
Establish the player's current reality on the field — from their perspective. Only 3–4 questions. Never assume you saw everything they saw. A good coach diagnoses before prescribing, just like a great physician.
Key rule: Never assume. What you saw from the sideline is incomplete. The player has information you don't — gather it before you coach it.
PDF principle: Diagnose before you prescribe. Every question here is intelligence gathering — not correction. Keep questions factual, neutral, and open.
Situation Diagnosis Scripts
"What coverage are they giving you when you line up on the right?"
"When you're in that position, what's your read telling you about their assignment?"
"How long have they been running that look — did it change after the second series?"
"What did their defensive end do on the last three pass plays from your side?"
Phase 03 · PDF Formula
Problem Awareness
Help players surface what isn't working — from their own mouth. When a player identifies the problem themselves, ownership transfers instantly. This is the most powerful phase in player development. No external identification = no internal change.
Key rule: Ask 3–6 questions. Go deeper on each answer. Don't answer for them. Silence after a question is not dead air — it's the player thinking. Let it breathe.
PDF principle: Pain is the catalyst. No felt problem = no motivation to change. Help them feel the gap between where they are and where they need to be — without manufacturing it.
Problem Awareness Scripts
"What's not working the way you expected it to out there?"
"Why do you think that's been happening — what's causing it?"
"How is that affecting the rest of your game when that breaks down?"
"Is there anything else that's been off that we haven't talked about yet?"
"How long have you noticed this pattern? Did it start today or has it been building?"
Phase 04 · PDF Formula
Solution Awareness
Let the player articulate what they think would fix it. When they describe the solution, they own it emotionally before you confirm it technically. This is the difference between a player who executes because they were told to and a player who executes because they decided to.
Key rule: Don't give your solution yet. Let them describe their ideal adjustment first. Even if their answer is partially wrong, the act of generating it creates buy-in you cannot manufacture.
PDF principle: Players persuade themselves. Your job in this phase is to hold space for their thinking, not fill it with yours.
Solution Awareness Scripts
"What do you think needs to change on your end to fix that?"
"If you could go back out there and do one thing differently right now, what would it be?"
"What would executing that correctly look like for you — walk me through it."
"What's stopped you from making that adjustment during the game so far?"
Phase 05 · PDF Formula
Consequence
Help the player think through what happens if nothing changes. This creates internal urgency without external pressure. Used sparingly — one or two questions maximum. Then silence. The player's imagination is more powerful than anything you could say.
Key rule: 1–2 questions only. Don't pile on. Ask the consequence question, then go silent. The player must sit in it for a moment — that discomfort is the catalyst for change.
PDF principle: Never use fear as a weapon — use consequence as a mirror. There is a difference. The goal is awareness, not shame.
Consequence Scripts
"If we keep running that same look and you keep getting the same result — what happens to the game?"
"Have you thought about what it costs the team if that breakdown keeps happening in the second half?"
"What happens to your spot on the field if this doesn't get corrected tonight?"
Phase 06 · PDF Formula
Transition
Bridge everything the player told you to the specific adjustment you're making. Do not pitch the play. Reflect their own words back to them, then connect your call to exactly what they identified. This is where trust is built under pressure.
PDF template: "Based on what you told me... [their logical problem]. And because of that it's costing us... [team impact]. What I'm putting in is [brief adjustment] — because it directly addresses what you just described. Does that feel right to you?"
Transition Scripts
"Based on what you told me — their safety is cheating down on your routes. That's exactly why I'm calling the seam. It's not a guess, it's your read. Go execute it."
"You just told me their corner is giving you a cushion. The adjustment we're making puts that cushion to work immediately. You've already identified it — now trust it."
"Everything you just described is exactly why we're making this switch. You saw it. I confirmed it. Go make it happen."
Phase 07 · PDF Formula
Present the Adjustment
Present your specific adjustment or play call as directly solving what the player identified. No feature dumps. No long explanations. This phase is 10% of the conversation — not 50%. Present only what addresses what they told you, confirm understanding, and move.
Key rule: Use checking-for-agreement questions constantly. "Does that make sense?" "Are we on the same page?" "Do you see how that hits what you described?" Every few sentences.
PDF principle: Old model: 50% presenting. PDF model: 10% presenting. The rest is diagnostic. If you're talking more than your player, you've lost the phase.
Presentation + Agreement Scripts
"Here's the adjustment — [specific call]. Does that make sense given what you're seeing?"
"We're going to [specific change]. Do you see how that directly answers the look you described?"
"I want you to [specific instruction]. What questions do you have?"
"Your job on this play is exactly one thing: [one instruction]. Can you do that?"
Phase 08 · PDF Formula
Commitment
Earn the player's commitment to the adjustment — don't assume it. A player who nods is not a player who commits. Ask them to articulate what they're going to do. When they say it, they own it. Execution rates after spoken commitment are dramatically higher than after passive instruction.
Key rule: Frame it as a natural next step they've already talked themselves into. They identified the problem. They suggested the fix. You confirmed it. Now ask them to own the execution.
PDF principle: Commitment is earned, not forced. The difference between "go do it" and "do you feel ready to execute that?" is the difference between compliance and conviction.
Commitment Scripts
"Do you feel confident you can execute that adjustment? Tell me why."
"Walk me through exactly what you're going to do differently on the next play."
"What are you going to do when you see that look again?"
"Are you ready to go make this happen? What's your first move when you get back out there?"
Phase 09 · PDF Formula
Handle Resistance
When players push back — "That won't work," "I can't make that read in time," "Coach, they're too fast" — never argue. Never overrule with authority. Use questions to help the player overcome their own resistance. Pushback is an unresolved concern from an earlier phase. The question is always the answer.
Key rule: Never rebut. Ask a question that returns the concern to the player. Help them solve their own hesitation. Authority-based overruling creates compliance without conviction — and breaks on the field.
PDF principle: Most resistance is preventable with earlier phases done correctly. If you're getting heavy pushback in phase 9, return to phase 3 and re-open the problem awareness conversation.
Resistance Handler Scripts
"I hear you — what specifically makes you feel like it won't work?"
"What would need to be different for you to feel confident running that?"
"Let me ask you this — if we don't make this adjustment, what do you think happens in the fourth quarter?"
"Have you run something like this before that felt similar? What happened then?"
"What's the worst that happens if you try it — and what's the worst that happens if we don't?"

The 6-Second Open

PHASE 02 · SITUATION
The Diagnosis Round
After each series, pull one player and ask only situation questions — zero instruction. Build the habit of gathering before giving. Measure how much information you missed before you started doing this.
PER SERIES
PHASE 03 · PROBLEM
The Mirror Drill
Ask a struggling player what's not working — then stay silent for 8 full seconds after they answer. The discomfort of the silence forces deeper self-reflection than any follow-up question.
HIGH IMPACT
PHASE 04 · SOLUTION
Let Them Call It
Once per practice, ask a player what adjustment they'd make before you tell them what you've decided. Even if their answer is different, the exercise builds tactical ownership that transfers to game execution.
WEEKLY
PHASE 05 · CONSEQUENCE
The Stakes Question
In film review, ask "What happens to the team if this continues?" before showing the correction. Document the answers. Players who can articulate consequences self-correct faster than players who are told.
FILM ROOM
PHASE 08 · COMMIT
The Spoken Commitment
End every tactical conversation by asking the player to say out loud exactly what they're going to do. Not "got it" — a full sentence. Studies show spoken commitment increases execution rate by 40%+ vs. passive acknowledgment.
